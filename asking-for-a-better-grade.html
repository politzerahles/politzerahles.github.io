How to ask for a better grade

At the end of each semester, professors* like me get lots of messages from students asking if there's anything they can do to increase their grade. Perhaps you are preparing to send a message like that to me or someone else; or perhaps you have recently sent one and been told "no". Either way, here is some advice on how to increase your chances of getting a positive response when you ask for a better grade.

The key to effectively asking about how to increase your grade is to understand what professors care about. So, in what follows, I'm going to talk about what professors care about and how it may differ from what you care about as a student; then I'll break down why certain sorts of requests (that don't address what professors care about) usually don't work; and finally, I'll provide some tips about how you can use what professors care about to frame your requests in a way that they are more likely to respond positively to.

What you care about

Before we talk about what professors care about, let's examine what you care about as a student, and how that informs the way you ask for better grades.

As a student, you probably care a lot about your grades. You are pretty much constantly surrounded by messages telling you how important grades are—that you need good grades to keep your funding, to secure good opportunities outside of school (whether those be employment, graduate study, fellowships, etc.), or even just to validate that you are a good student. Some of this messaging may be true and some may not be; some of the importance of grades may be a good thing and some may be a bad thing; but I am not here to make judgments about that right now. The bottom line is, whether it's a good or bad, whether it's right or wrong, the reality is that you are in a situation where you have plenty of reason to feel that your grades are very important. Your professors realize that. All this is to say, if you ask for a better grade, I get where you're coming from.

But when you use this as the justification for requesting a better grade, I imagine you probably are not often successful. Imagine you send an e-mail at the end of the semester asking your professor if there's anything you can do to bump your grade up from a C to a B, because you need a B to keep your financial aid. Does this work? I imagine that a lot of professors in that situation would tell you, "If you knew that you needed a B, you should have worked hard throughout the semester to make sure you got a B; at this point it's too late to do anything." Maybe you have even gotten a response like that before; I know I've sent my own fair share of judgy e-mails like that.

Why do requests like this usually not work? Because they are focused on what you want or need, and neglect what your professor cares about. If you want to have more success in asking for better grades or for ways to increase your grade, you should understand what professors do care about, and how to use that to your advantage. So let's turn to that now.

What professors care about

This might come as a surprise, but most of your professors do not care about grades. They care about your learning. Different professors have different views of grades, but these views are always based on how the grades relate to your learning.

I would roughly organize professors into four groups, based on how they view grades. Two of these groups like grades, one feels neutral about them, and one dislikes them.** But what they all have in common is that they do not care about grades for grades' own sake; they only care about how grades relate to your learning. To better understand how your professors might think about grades, let's take a closer look at each of these groups.

Grade users – "Grades are a tool"

These professors use grades as an incentive to get you to learn. They start by thinking of some task or project that they would like you to do because they believe it will get you to learn something (for example, maybe they want you to conduct an experiment because it will help you learn how to do research; maybe they want you to write a computer program because it will help you get practice in using certain software design principles; maybe they just want you to come to class, because you'll learn better if you're in class). They attach a grade to it to motivate you to do the work. These professors probably have a positive outlook on grades, but not because they "like" grades in of themselves; they only like grades insofar as grades can be a useful tool to get you to learn. The grade is not the goal, it's just a means to an end.

Grade communicators – "Grades are information"

There is another kind of professor who has a pretty positive outlook towards grades: those who value grades as a tool to measure learning and to improve classes. These professors might not use grades as a carrot or stick to get you to learn like the abovementioned group does, but these professors do use grades as a form of feedback for themselves or their departments. They look at grades to figure out what students are doing well at and not so well at, and they then use that to revamp classes and better ensure that students are learning what they should learn. They also view grades as a way to communicate to stakeholders outside the university (such as potential employers) about how well you've done. These professors, like the group described above, are using grades as a tool, albeit for a different thing: they're not using grades as a tool to make you learn, but as a tool to improve their classes and to document your performance.

Grade tolerators – "Grades are a chore"

These professors give you a grade because they have to. They want you to learn and that's what they really care about, but they assign grades to that learning because the university requires it. Grades are something they could take or leave; as long as you are learning, that's what these professors care about, and they would probably be perfectly happy never giving a grade if they weren't required to. Some of these professors pretty much treat grades as an afterthought. Others, however, do put a lot of thought into how they design tests, assessments, and rubrics, and they often have very well thought-out grading schemes. But even when their grading scheme is very well designed, they aren't doing it because they cherish the sanctity of what a B- means or anything like that; they are doing it because grading is just something they have to do.

Grade haters – "Grades are a barrier"

These professors believe that grades are antithetical to learning. They think that the mere existence of grades stifles learning and encourages behaviour that is not conducive to learning, while at the same time discouraging the sort of risk-taking and exploration that leads to true learning. They believe that learning would be better if there were no grades. These professors may be part of the growing "ungrading" or "going gradeless" movement. They might still put a grade onto your transcript at the end of the semester (because, as discussed above, they are usually required to), but they don't design the whole class around grades, and they do everything they can to minimize what they see as the harms of grading, while putting off all the grade talk as long as possible.

Why asking "How can I improve my grade?" doesn't work

As we have seen above, your professors don't care about your grade. Some of them think grades are a useful tool for incentivizing learning or for documenting your performance; some think grades are a chore they have to get through; and some think grades are a scourge. But what they all have in common is that none of them care about grades for grades' own sake. At best, they think grades are something they can use to get you to learn; at the other extreme, some professors actively want to discourage you from focusing on grades.

Let's link these ideas back to the example we started with—a scenario in which you approach a professor at the end of the semester to ask if there's anything you can do to increase your grade from a C to a B because you really need a B. How would each kind of professor think about that request?

The grade users will not be interested, because what you're asking to do is not something that would accomplish what those professors want. If a professor uses grades to get you to learn, then they will feel that a last-minute request to increase your grade is not something that's going to lead to learning. For them, the whole point of the grade was to get you to do some work that would cause you to think deeply and learn. Something you hurry to submit at the end of the semester just to get a grade is probably not going to lead to that sort of learning. Giving you a higher grade just because you need it would undercut their purpose for using grades which was to get you to learn something.

Likewise, the grade communicators will think that changing your grade is going to throw off the accuracy of the measurement they want to get from your grade. If they want the grade to measure how well you're learning, then they would not want to muddy it by letting it be influenced by factors unrelated to learning, such as how much you need a B. They want grades to measure learning, so if any factor other than learning (such as personal need) is influencing the grade, then the grade is doing a worse job of measuring learning. So, once again, these kinds of professors are not likely to want to give you a higher grade just because you want or need it.

The grade tolerators will not be interested because they aren't particularly interested in grades, they are interested in what you're learning and how well you are learning it. By asking them to figure out ways to increase your grade, you are pretty much asking them to spend more time thinking about something they already don't want to be thinking about. The fact that they don't want to think about it doesn't necessarily mean they won't think about it—thinking about grades is, after all, usually part of their job—but if your request only focuses on how much you want or need a better grade then you are really not putting your best foot forward in how you approach these professors.

The grade haters will react in much the same way as the grade tolerators, because they too care more about what you're learning than about what grade you're getting. When you come to them asking for a better grade, you are focusing precisely on what they want you to not be focusing on. It's not even just that they themselves don't want to be thinking about grades; they actively want to get you to stop worrying about grades, too, because they believe you will learn better once you are freed from the oppressive shackles of the grades mindset.

No matter what sort of professor you have, just asking them how you can increase your grade is never likely to get a good response—especially if you're doing this at the very end of the term. When you just ask how to increase your grade, you are not focusing on what the professor cares about and wants you to care about.

Using what professors care about to frame your request

So now you understand what your professors care about: they don't care about your grade, they care about how well you are learning. You also now understand why just asking about how to increase your grade is not likely to fly with most professors. But how can you do better?

If you know what professors want, you can frame your request in terms of those things. A professor doesn't care if you "get a good grade", they care if you learn. So, instead of asking for a better grade, ask for an opportunity to learn better. Instead of telling them how much you want or need a better grade, tell them why a better grade would better reflect what you have learned. Let's take a closer look at each of these strategies.

Strategy #1: Another chance to learn

Most professors would rather see you learn than see you get a bad grade. So, if you bombed a test or did poorly in a project, show that you understand where and how your work fell short, and show that you are interested in trying to do a better job and learning the thing more deeply than what your previous work showed. That may involve having another chance to do it, or it may involve an alternative assessment that would push you to learn what you didn't learn while doing the original assessment. I can't guarantee that every professor will give you this opportunity (we are busy people and don't always have the time to design a whole new learning opportunity for you), but I can guarantee that you will succeed more often by framing this as a request for another chance to learn, rather than a request for a higher grade. This is especially more likely to work if you demonstrate a willingness to meet your professor halfway and work together to figure out something you can do, rather than just asking them "Is there anything I can do?"

Strategy #2: Another way to demonstrate what you've learned

Most professors are also open to the idea that their grades might not accurately reflect what you have learned. For example, I teach linguistics, and I am much better at recognizing good ideas about linguistics than I am at designing tests and rubrics. I have many years of experience and expertise doing and evaluating linguistics research, and I simply do not have as much experience and expertise at things like curriculum design and pedagogy. Furthermore, as an educator, I am always thinking critically about how I am assessing my students' learning and whether I can do a better job of it. Throughout my years of teaching, I have had some students who do well on paper and get good grades but who really don't impress me at all when I interact with them some time after the class is over; and, on the flip side, I have had others who didn't get good grades and don't look good on paper but who have surprised me with deep and critical insights someday later. The upshot of all that is, I am deeply aware of the possibility that the way I'm grading you might not be doing a good job reflecting how much you've really learned. And most professors are just like me in this respect. If you tell a professor that you think you have learned a certain topic pretty well but that the grade you got on that assignment didn't reflect how well you've learned, and that you are interested in exploring other options to prove what you know, then many professors will be receptive to this. Hippie professors like me eat that shit up.

Additional tips: Fake it 'til you make it, and address this early

I am not saying you need to believe in your heart of hearts that you really want to learn the stuff. You might think everything I've said so far is hippie B.S.; maybe you really just want the grade and don't care about all this touchy-feely "learning" nonsense. Still, if you want that grade, just fake it. Pretend that you want to learn better, or pretend that you just really want to show the professor how much you've learned. That will increase your chance of getting the better grade; furthermore, if the professor does take you up on this and works with you to figure out an opportunity for you to earn a better grade, then you might actually learn something from the process, even if that wasn't your main goal. You get what you wanted—the better grade—and the professor gets what they wanted—you learning something. It's a win-win for everyone!

Finally, for any of this to work, it needs to happen early. Learning takes time; hammering out an extra project in the last couple days before grades are due is not likely to lead to any real learning. Likewise, figuring out other ways to show your learning takes time, and a professor who has lots of final papers to grade already will probably not have enough time to sit down with you and figure out alternative assessments. Finally, talking to your professor early makes both of your lives easier, because if you're having this discussion in the middle of the semester then you might not even need to figure out alternative ways of learning and assessing your learning; you might be able to just talk about ways to do better on the things that already planned (e.g., upcoming tests or projects), which is generally a lot easier than figuring out something new.

For either of the strategies I have described above to be convincing, you should be approaching your professor long before the end of the semester—the middle of the semester is ideal—to start a dialogue about this. If you're in finals week now and thinking about sending that e-mail, then you probably will not have much success and there's probably nothing left you can do about this class—but at least now you know about these strategies for next semester.

Conclusion: don't ask for a better grade, ask to co-create a learning opportunity

So, what's the best way to ask for a better grade? The short answer is: don't. At least, not for its own sake. What you should really ask for is another chance to learn what you need to learn, or another chance to demonstrate what you have learned, or both. Either of those might also result in a better grade, but the grade itself is not the point; learning, and showing your learning, is the point. Again, I cannot guarantee that this will work on every professor or in every class,*** but I am confident it will work far more often than "how can I increase my grade?"; at the very least, professors will be more willing to work with you if you approach them the way I have described here.

*I am using the term "professor" loosely to refer to the instructors of any university-level classes; technically, not all of them are professors, but asking them for a better grade works the same way as asking professors for a better grade.

**In reality things are not so clear-cut; your professors are humans and have complex personalities just like anyone else, so they probably all exhibit some features of each of these groups, or may even shift between different grade philosophies from time to time or from class to class.

***Another issue I haven't addressed at all is fairness. Some professors may not want to grant you this request, even if you use one of my strategies to frame it, because they might be concerned that it's not fair to other students who don't get the same arrangements as you are asking for. I don't have any special tips for satisfying that kind of professor, but here my advice about timing still applies: the earlier you broach this subject with your professor, the better chance they'll be willing and able to find a way to work with you that they will think is fair.
